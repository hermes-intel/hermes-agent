# Hermes ID — Scoring Rubric

Detailed criteria for each of the 8 AI proficiency dimensions. Use this rubric to ensure consistent, evidence-based scoring.

## General Principles

- **Evidence-based:** Every score must be supported by specific tweet content. No speculation.
- **Recency matters:** More recent tweets carry slightly more weight (AI moves fast).
- **Quality over quantity:** One deep technical thread > ten shallow mentions.
- **Context-aware:** A researcher sharing a paper insight scores higher on Understanding than someone retweeting a paper link without commentary.

## Dimension Details

### 1. AI Usage (weight: 15%)

How frequently and broadly does the user actually use AI tools in their work/life?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Daily, multi-tool AI user. Mentions 5+ different AI tools/models. Shows screenshots/outputs from AI tools. Discusses specific workflows integrating AI. Compares tools for specific tasks. |
| 75–89 | Regular AI user. Mentions 3-4 tools. Shares AI-generated outputs. Discusses how AI fits into their workflow. |
| 60–74 | Moderate use. Mentions 1-2 tools regularly. Some evidence of actual use beyond curiosity. |
| 40–59 | Occasional mentions. Talks about AI tools but limited evidence of hands-on use. |
| 20–39 | Rare mentions. Mostly observational — "AI is interesting" level. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of AI tool usage. |

**What to look for:** Tool names (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Midjourney, Cursor, GitHub Copilot, Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, Whisper, Runway, Suno, ElevenLabs), model names (GPT-4o, Claude Opus, Llama 3, Mistral, Gemma), API mentions, "I asked Claude to...", screenshots of AI outputs, "Using X for Y" patterns.

### 2. AI Understanding (weight: 15%)

How deeply do they understand how AI actually works?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Discusses architecture details (transformer internals, attention mechanisms, MoE, diffusion). References specific papers. Understands training methods (RLHF, DPO, GRPO, SFT). Talks about scaling laws, emergent abilities, tokenization, quantization. |
| 75–89 | Shows solid technical knowledge. Discusses benchmarks meaningfully. Understands model families and their differences. Can explain why one model outperforms another. |
| 60–74 | Moderate technical awareness. Knows the difference between model sizes, understands fine-tuning conceptually, follows AI research developments. |
| 40–59 | Surface-level understanding. Knows major model names but doesn't discuss internals. Retweets technical content without adding insight. |
| 20–39 | Minimal technical awareness. Might confuse terminology. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of technical understanding. |

**What to look for:** Architecture names, paper titles/IDs, benchmark names (MMLU, HumanEval, MATH, ARC), training methodology terms, inference optimization (KV cache, speculative decoding, quantization), parameter counts, context window discussions.

### 3. Communication (weight: 12%)

How well do they engage with the broader AI community?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Central node in AI Twitter. Regularly tagged in discussions. Quality replies to researchers and builders. Threads get significant engagement. Bridges different AI subcommunities. |
| 75–89 | Active and respected in AI conversations. Thoughtful replies. Gets meaningful engagement on AI posts. |
| 60–74 | Regularly engages in AI discussions. Some quality interactions, but not a focal point. |
| 40–59 | Occasional AI discussion. Mostly retweets or brief comments. |
| 20–39 | Rare engagement with AI content. Mostly passive consumer. |
| 0–19 | No AI community engagement visible. |

**What to look for:** Reply quality (adding insight vs "great post!"), engagement metrics, being quote-tweeted by AI practitioners, participating in AI debates, connecting ideas across conversations.

### 4. Product Building (weight: 15%)

Do they actually build things with AI?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Ships AI-powered products. Shares working demos. Links to GitHub repos. Builds agents, tools, extensions. Shows iterative development. Multiple shipped projects. |
| 75–89 | Active builder. At least one visible AI project. Shares development progress. Code snippets. Technical implementation details. |
| 60–74 | Has built something with AI. Shares project updates occasionally. Some evidence of hands-on development. |
| 40–59 | Talks about wanting to build. Maybe one small experiment. More talk than ship. |
| 20–39 | Mentions building with AI but no visible output. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of building. |

**What to look for:** GitHub links, demo videos, "I built...", project announcements, deployment discussions, bug reports from their own AI tools, architecture decisions, "shipped" / "launched" language, open-source contributions.

### 5. Adoption Speed (weight: 12%)

How quickly do they jump on new AI releases?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Consistently among the first to try new models/tools. Tweets about new releases within hours. Beta/preview access. Testing experimental features on day one. |
| 75–89 | Early adopter. Tries major new releases within days. Shares first impressions before most people. |
| 60–74 | Stays current. Tries new things within a week or two. Follows release announcements. |
| 40–59 | Moderate follower. Aware of new releases but doesn't rush to try them. |
| 20–39 | Late adopter. Mentions tools/models weeks or months after release. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of tracking new releases. |

**What to look for:** Timestamps relative to known release dates, "just dropped" / "day one" / "first look" language, API waitlist mentions, early access, comparing new vs previous versions immediately.

### 6. Prompt Engineering (weight: 10%)

How sophisticated are their prompting skills?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Shares advanced prompt techniques. System prompt design. Chain-of-thought, few-shot, structured output formatting. Meta-prompting. Prompt optimization. Discusses prompt-model interaction dynamics. |
| 75–89 | Good prompt awareness. Shares effective prompts. Understands prompt structure beyond basic queries. |
| 60–74 | Some evidence of prompt craft. Occasionally shares prompts or tips. |
| 40–59 | Basic prompting. Uses AI tools but no evidence of prompt sophistication. |
| 20–39 | Minimal prompt awareness. |
| 0–19 | No evidence. |

**What to look for:** Shared prompt text, "system prompt", "few-shot", "chain of thought", "structured output", prompt templates, "I got better results by...", prompt comparison/testing, discussion of prompt strategies.

### 7. Critical Awareness (weight: 10%)

Do they understand AI's limitations and risks?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Nuanced, balanced perspective. Discusses hallucinations, bias, safety, and alignment with depth. Understands when AI fails and why. Questions hype with evidence. Considers societal impact. |
| 75–89 | Good critical thinking about AI. Mentions limitations. Balanced view of capabilities vs risks. |
| 60–74 | Some awareness of limitations. Occasionally notes when AI gets things wrong. |
| 40–59 | Minimal critical perspective. Mostly positive/hype without nuance. |
| 20–39 | Uncritical AI enthusiasm. No mention of limitations. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of critical thinking about AI. |

**What to look for:** "Hallucination", "bias", "alignment", "safety", "limitations", "AI can't do X", "be careful with AI for Y", balanced takes, failure mode discussion, responsible AI practices, data privacy concerns.

### 8. Knowledge Sharing (weight: 11%)

Do they actively teach others about AI?

| Score Range | Criteria |
|-------------|----------|
| 90–100 | Prolific AI educator. Publishes tutorials, tip threads, guides. Explains complex concepts clearly. Creates learning resources. Mentors others. Multiple educational threads. |
| 75–89 | Regular knowledge sharer. Several educational posts. Explains concepts to followers. Answers questions helpfully. |
| 60–74 | Occasional teaching. Shares tips or explanations now and then. |
| 40–59 | Rare educational content. Mostly shares for personal context, not teaching. |
| 20–39 | Almost no teaching/sharing intent. |
| 0–19 | No evidence of knowledge sharing. |

**What to look for:** "How to...", "Tutorial:", "Thread:", "Tips for...", step-by-step explanations, answering follower questions, recommending resources, "here's what I learned", beginner-friendly explanations of advanced topics.

## Level Definitions

| Level | Score | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| Agent God | 96–100 | Pinnacle of AI mastery. Builds, teaches, innovates, and shapes the field. |
| AI Native | 86–95 | AI is fully integrated into their identity and work. Deep and broad proficiency. |
| AI Expert | 71–85 | Strong, well-rounded AI skills. Clearly ahead of the curve. |
| AI Practitioner | 51–70 | Active AI user and learner. Solid foundation, room to grow. |
| AI Curious | 31–50 | Beginning their AI journey. Interested but limited depth. |
| AI Newbie | 0–30 | Little to no AI engagement visible in their public content. |

## Weighted Total Calculation

```
total = (AI_Usage * 0.15) + (AI_Understanding * 0.15) + (Communication * 0.12)
      + (Product_Building * 0.15) + (Adoption_Speed * 0.12) + (Prompt_Engineering * 0.10)
      + (Critical_Awareness * 0.10) + (Knowledge_Sharing * 0.11)
```

Round to the nearest integer.
